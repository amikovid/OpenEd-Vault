## Summary

## Content

In the late 1800s, as England built its first compulsory education system, Victorian educator Charlotte Mason gave parents a pressure valve: permission to trust themselves.

This was as radical in her time as it is in ours - perhaps more so. The message of compulsory schooling was clear: leave teaching to the professionals. Mason disagreed. Children are born persons, she said - not blank slates waiting for experts to inscribe knowledge. They're capable of engaging with real ideas from the start. And parents who know their children are capable of guiding that journey.

Mason wrote six volumes on education, founded the Parents' National Educational Union, and trained teachers at her college in Ambleside, England. But she never created a curriculum.

A Google search for "Charlotte Mason curriculum" returns millions of results - detailed book lists, year-by-year schedules, scope and sequences. Some guides get specific: "you *must* read Plutarch's *Lives*," they tell you, or, "you *have to* include this particular sequence of history." These guides can be helpful starting points. But we have to remember that they're interpretations of Mason's philosophy, not the philosophy itself.

Plutarch himself - a Greek historian and philosopher - would probably object to being made into "mandatory reading." To Plutarch the quote is often attributed that, "the mind is not a vessel to be filled, but a fire to be kindled." The original is even better: "The correct analogy for the mind is not a vessel that needs filling, but wood that needs igniting - no more - and then it motivates one towards originality and instills the desire for truth."

That's the spirit Charlotte Mason was after. Not a checklist to complete, but a fire to tend.

Her core principles share more in common with [open education](https://opened.co/open-education) than with any packaged curriculum: the world is the best classroom, you don't need to get everything from one place, and children learn best when they're genuinely engaged with real ideas.

But one principle stands out in particular for its compatibility with our ethos at OpenEd: teach the child, not the grade level.

As [Sonya Schafer of Simply Charlotte Mason](https://simplycharlottemason.com/) puts it:

> "It's very common for a child to be ready for, say, third-grade math skills while at the same time be working on second-grade or even first-grade reading skills. That's fine!"

Over a century ago, Miss Mason understood that kids don't develop like cogs on an assembly line. Grade levels persist not because they're good for kids but because they're [efficient for institutions](https://opened.co/blog/beyond-mere-choice).

If you've been interested in Charlotte Mason but afraid it will require a mid-year curriculum overhaul, take heart: you can be inspired by Charlotte Mason without going all in on her method. You can take the micro-lessons but skip the nature journals. You can explore older "living books" while sticking with modern Singapore Math. You can follow her philosophy without following someone else's book list.

There is no "everybody needs to..." but there are certain core principles you can adapt to fit your family.

## The Charlotte Mason Method

Mason's 19th-century Victorian prose can feel intimidating - references to governesses, nurseries, and yes, Plutarch. It sometimes reads like it was designed for wealthy families with household staff.

But it wasn't. Thankfully, we have a whole generation of modern interpreters making these ideas accessible. [Leah Boden](https://www.leahboden.com/), author of *Modern Miss Mason*, does for Charlotte Mason what the *No Fear Shakespeare* editions do for the Bard - she translates Victorian-era philosophy for modern families without dumbing it down. And as Simply Charlotte Mason demonstrates in their guide "[Charlotte Mason Without Breaking the Bank](https://simplycharlottemason.com/blog/charlotte-mason-on-a-budget/)," a rich education doesn't require a big budget. Library books, smart sharing, and free online resources can take you far.

Furthermore, don't be fooled by the picture-perfect images of Charlotte Mason setups on Instagram. [Jean Lee](https://theacademyofchaos.com/), a homeschool coach and microschool founder, put it this way on the OpenEd podcast:

> "That picture you see on Instagram of a beautiful Charlotte Mason family sitting and reading a book... That's not reality. It only lasted for three minutes, and then the baby started crawling away, and the next little one started screaming."

The core principles are simpler than the packaging suggests:

**1. Children are born persons.** They aren't beakers to fill or problems to manage. They are whole people, worthy of respect, capable of engaging with real ideas from the start.

**2. Education is the science of relations.** Learning happens when children form connections - to ideas, nature, art, history, literature. The goal is helping children relate to the world, not stuffing facts into heads.

**3. Living books over "twaddle."** Charlotte Mason had a word for watered-down, dumbed-down content that talks down to children: *twaddle*. Textbooks that oversimplify. Abridged versions that strip the life out of literature. Bright colors and cartoon characters substituting for substance.

The antidote is what she called "living books" - real literature written by someone passionate about their subject, not a committee producing content for grade-level benchmarks. As education researcher [Kerry McDonald](https://www.liberatedpodcast.com/) observes, "Some families follow Charlotte Mason's literature-rich philosophy; others embrace Wild + Free's focus on nature and wonder." The Wild + Free podcast captures this blend - what some call a "Charlotte Mason-meets-unschooling vibe" for families who value muddy boots, story-rich days, and the wonder of childhood.

**4. Short lessons beat long ones.** Fifteen to twenty minutes for young children. Focus intensely, then move on. This keeps attention sharp and prevents the grinding fatigue of hour-long blocks.

Homeschool expert Pam Barnhill developed what she calls [the 10-Minute Rule](https://opened.co/blog/the-10-minute-rule): set a timer for 10 minutes of intense focus on a subject and stop immediately when it goes off. No dragging it out. No "just finish this page." The timer is the boss.

Likewise, OpenEd Chief Academic Officer [Andrea Fife](https://opened.co/blog/episode-005-she-almost-gave-up-on-education) recommends keeping lessons to 20-30 minute blocks with movement breaks for learners who struggle with sustained attention.

**5. Narration.** Instead of worksheets and tests, children retell what they learned. You can't narrate what you didn't absorb. This forces genuine understanding.

**6. A wide and generous curriculum.** There's no hierarchy of "core" versus "extracurriculars." Art, music, poetry, handicrafts, and nature study matter as much as reading and math. Charlotte Mason called this spreading a "feast" - expose children to many things and see what they reach for.

If this sounds like something you're already doing - or an approach you're drawn to - read on.

## Charlotte Mason by Subject

Here's how those principles play out in practice.

### Reading and Literature

Here, living books are the foundation. Instead of a textbook about colonial America, you might read *Johnny Tremain* - a Newbery Medal novel about a silversmith's apprentice caught up in the Revolution. Instead of a sanitized biography, you might read actual letters and journals. The difference is palpable: a child who groans at a textbook will often beg for one more chapter of a living book.

[Sonya Shafer](https://simplycharlottemason.com/) recommends the "[cliffhanger technique](https://opened.co/blog/the-cliffhanger-technique-for-read-alouds)" for family read-alouds: stop at the good parts, not at chapter ends. This builds anticipation. Children actually want to come back.

For phonics and early reading, [Rachael Davie](https://opened.co/blog/are-we-teaching-stem-backwards), OpenEd's curriculum lead, calls the Charlotte Mason-inspired [All About Reading](https://www.allaboutlearningpress.com/all-about-reading/) a "fabulous phonics curriculum" that uses a "multi-sensory, mastery-based approach" - it follows Orton-Gillingham principles without the hefty price tag of formal programs.

### Writing and Composition

Charlotte Mason believed writing should emerge naturally from narration. "Composition should not be taught," she wrote. "It should be the outcome of [spontaneous interest](https://opened.co/blog/breaking-writing-rules-with-purpose-127)."

Daily narration - retelling what you've read or learned - builds toward essay writing without formal instruction. By the time a child needs to write, they've been composing orally for years. Copywork and dictation reinforce spelling and grammar through exposure to good writing rather than drilling rules.

OpenEd mentor teacher Erica Michaelis recommends [IEW's](https://opened.co/tools/institute-for-excellence-in-writing-iew) Fix-it Grammar for a gentle approach: "Each day you fix up a sentence or two. The student reads the lesson, then fixes the sentences while learning about grammar topics. It takes 10-15 min and introduces grammar concepts through the editing process."

### Math

The [Simply Charlotte Mason podcast](https://simplycharlottemason.com/blog/the-beauty-of-math/) offers a liberating perspective: "Anytime you're taking something that's chaotic and you're bringing order to it, that's math."

You find math in bird calls, photography composition, organizing a messy shelf. "We don't have to consider ourselves mathematicians to enjoy math."

That said, if your child struggles with math, you don't have to stay pure. Tools like Singapore Math or Right Start work well alongside Charlotte Mason methods. The philosophy doesn't require one-size-fits-all.

### Handwriting

Charlotte Mason's approach to handwriting mirrors her philosophy everywhere else: slow, methodical, and habitual. She recommended starting with simple strokes before even teaching letter formation. Short lessons - five to ten minutes - with the expectation of careful work for that brief time.

Some families use italic handwriting programs like Penny Gardner's "Italics: Beautiful Handwriting for Children," which naturally progresses into cursive. But if handwriting becomes a battle, programs like Handwriting Without Tears work fine alongside this approach.

### Nature Study

Miss Mason didn't treat nature as a subject to study but as an actual teacher. Children observe. They sketch what they see. They record the date, the weather, what caught their eye.

[Whitney Newby](https://www.instagram.com/brighterdaypress/) of Brighter Day Press has built a following around poetry and nature-focused Charlotte Mason resources - an accessible bridge for families who want the philosophy without the Victorian overwhelm.

Over time, nature study builds something no curriculum can manufacture: the habit of noticing. A child who has spent years watching seasons change and birds migrate sees the world differently than one who hasn't.

### Art and Music Appreciation

Picture study: show a child a great painting for five to ten minutes. Let them look without commentary. Then ask: what did you notice?

This builds what Charlotte Mason called "visual vocabulary." The child looks at a painting for several minutes, then describes it from memory. This trains observation skills that transfer to science, writing, and every other subject. [Autumn Kern](https://www.thecommonplacepodcast.com/) explores this emphasis on beauty and slow observation in her Charlotte Mason work.

Jen Johnson, OpenEd's Curriculum Team Lead, recommends The Great Artist Program because it offers "short lessons (30-45 minutes) combining an artist biography with a hands-on project." Resources like [A Humble Place](https://ahumbleplace.com/product/bierstadt-picture-study/) provide the paintings and stories behind them. But a library book of art prints works too.

### Habit Training

This is where Charlotte Mason gets practical - and where her method connects to the everyday reality of running a household.

[Meg Thomas](https://coachmegthomas.com/), a homeschool coach and mother of seven, discovered that habits come before curriculum. She taught her children household habits first - doing laundry together as a "full family event," making beds, cleaning up after meals.

"I didn't realize that these skills I was teaching them was going to help me to make homeschool feasible long-term," Meg explains. "I basically am working myself out of a job."

Her framework - Heart (connection), Habits, then Home (culture) - mirrors Charlotte Mason's emphasis on habit training: when children know what's expected and have internalized good habits, the daily friction disappears. You're not fighting battles over basics. The home runs smoothly, and learning flows.

This is pure Charlotte Mason - habits before academics.

## The Charlotte Mason Curriculum Landscape

You might find a curriculum that fits, or you might realize you don't need one at all.

### Free and Low-Cost Options

[**Ambleside Online**](https://opened.co/tools/ambleside-online) is free, rigorous, and community-developed. It's the closest thing to a purist Charlotte Mason curriculum - detailed book lists, schedules, active forums. Christian worldview throughout. If you want the full experience without spending money, start here.

**Your library card** covers most living books. A nature journal and some colored pencils handle nature study. The philosophy is the curriculum.

[**Simply Charlotte Mason**](https://simplycharlottemason.com/) offers practical guidance for free through their blog and podcast, even if you never buy their materials.

### Christian and Religious Curricula

**Important note:** OpenEd cannot reimburse religious curricula.

Most established Charlotte Mason curricula come from a Christian perspective.

**Simply Charlotte Mason** is the most accessible entry point. Sonya Shafer has built a gentle, supportive community around the method. Christian but not heavily doctrinal.

**The Alveary** is perhaps the most academically demanding option. Heavy on original sources and languages. For families who want rigor and challenge.

### Secular and Flexible Options

[**Blossom & Root**](https://blossomandroot.com/) is nature-focused and literature-based with beautiful design. Secular, though some families note occasional "nature spirituality" language. OpenEd mentor Juliann Akers uses it extensively: "I LOVE Blossom and Root. We have used it for History, Science, and Nature Study. I recommend starting with their Early Years bundle - it covers Science and Nature Study and is gentle and laid back." She notes one caveat: "Just know that you'll have to buy the books separately, which can get pricey. Libraries help a lot."

[**Build Your Library**](https://buildyourlibrary.com/) is secular and literature-heavy, more eclectic than a pure Charlotte Mason approach. Good for families who want curated book lists without philosophy lectures.

[**The Well-Educated Heart**](https://opened.co/tools/well-educated-heart), created by Marlene Peterson, offers a CM-influenced approach with its own distinct philosophy - "heart then mind" - emphasizing character formation before academics. Her Libraries of Hope project provides free access to thousands of living books.

## FAQ

**Is Charlotte Mason only for Christians?**

The original writings are Christian. The principles are universal. Secular families adapt freely, and curricula like Blossom & Root apply these methods without religious content.

**Do I need to buy a curriculum?**

No. Charlotte Mason is a philosophy. Library books, free Ambleside booklists, nature journals, and public domain resources can take you far.

**How is Charlotte Mason different from Classical education?**

Both value great books and liberal arts. But [Classical education](https://opened.co/open-education/classical-education) follows the Trivium - grammar, logic, and rhetoric stages corresponding roughly to elementary, middle, and high school. Charlotte Mason rejected stage-based thinking. She believed children of any age can engage with sophisticated ideas.

Classical can be drill-heavy: Latin conjugations, memory work, recitation. The Charlotte Mason approach emphasizes short lessons, narration, and nature study. Classical often feels more rigorous and academic. Charlotte Mason feels gentler and more organic.

**How is Charlotte Mason different from **[**unschooling**](https://opened.co/blog/unschooling-a-guide-to-child-led-interest-driven-learning)**?**

Charlotte Mason recommends more structure than pure unschooling - scheduled lessons, required subjects, expectations about what children will encounter. But she shares the unschooling belief that children learn through interest and experience.

Her "wide and generous curriculum" - exposing children to many subjects without deciding in advance which ones matter - echoes the unschooling practice of "strewing" ideas and materials. Many families blend both approaches: Charlotte Mason structure for some subjects, child-led exploration for others.

**What ages does Charlotte Mason work for?**

Originally designed for ages 6-18. Many families adapt it for younger children, though Miss Mason herself advocated for play-based early childhood. Formal academics before age six weren't part of her vision.

**Can I mix Charlotte Mason with other approaches?**

Yes. Singapore Math for a child who struggles with Charlotte Mason math approaches. Handwriting Without Tears when penmanship becomes a battle. Interest-led projects alongside scheduled lessons. The method is a philosophy, not a prison.
