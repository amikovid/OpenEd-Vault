    [Having a purpose](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/The-Path-to-Purpose/William-Damon/9781416537243) comes with benefits, according to [Heather Malin](https://twitter.com/heathermalin), a consultant and former director of research at the [Stanford Center on Adolescence](https://coa.stanford.edu/).     So how can schools tap into the [power of purpose](https://www.kqed.org/mindshift/57946/striving-or-thriving-steps-to-help-kids-find-balance-and-purpose)? In some of her research that is not yet published, alumni of purpose learning programs have reported improved relationships with teachers and peers.
    
    Malin identified three dimensions of purpose that teachers can help students explore:
    
    - **Generalized and meaningful intention** ‚Äì This involves the student‚Äôs understanding of who they want to be in the world and informs how they set goals.
    - **Beyond-the-self motivation** ‚Äì Engaging students in issues outside their own lived experiences and helping them find ways to contribute empowers them with the skills and mindset to live a purposeful life.
    - **Goal-directed action** ‚Äì Because [future planning is challenging for teens](https://www.kqed.org/mindshift/57424/the-two-types-of-independence-growing-tweens-need-to-practice), they need support in learning to set and work toward goals.
    
    Teen brains are neurologically wired to [seek excitement and new stimuli](https://www.kqed.org/mindshift/61026/10-things-to-know-about-how-social-media-affects-teens-brains). ‚ÄúIf we can align school learning activity to that exploratory process, we have a powerful way to motivate teens,‚Äù Malin said.     ## Step 1: Self-exploration
    
    The first step in the purpose learning framework is inviting students to self-discovery with questions such as:
    
    - Are there any societal or cultural influences that have shaped your values?
    - How well do you think your family of origins values align with your personal values?
    
    Self-exploration discussions allow students to [foster deeper connections with their peers](https://www.kqed.org/mindshift/62649/mental-health-tools-for-tweens-by-phyllis-fagell) and teachers that can allow for deeper learning. ‚Äú[The students] didn‚Äôt realize they had so many shared experiences, challenges, hopes and worries,‚Äù she said regarding [student feedback](https://www.kqed.org/mindshift/60120/helicopter-teaching-how-using-student-feedback-can-help-with-that) she and her team received during research.
    
    ## Step 2: Look outward
    
    Prompting students to look outward, the second step of the framework, can help them to define their own sense of purpose. ‚ÄúSelf-awareness is important, but it‚Äôs meaningless if we have no idea how to connect our inner self to the world beyond ourselves,‚Äù said Malin.     ## Step 3: Future planning
    
    The third step of the purpose learning framework is engaging students in forward thinking and future planning. ‚ÄúThe mechanics of looking forward, looking to the future with a goal setting and planning approach can be much more challenging for adolescents,‚Äù so it‚Äôs important that teachers scaffold these forward looking steps for students, said Malin.
    
    ## Step 4: Action
    
    When it comes to taking action, the fourth step of Malin‚Äôs framework, educators can encourage students to do meaningful work outside the classroom and in their community.     Malin advocates for a less traditional approach to learning that is more aligned to adolescent brains. ‚ÄúIf we want to really motivate students in school, we need to be giving them opportunities to have an impact beyond the classroom.‚Äù According to Malin, one way to do this is by giving educators opportunities to collaborate and connect. ‚ÄúWe‚Äôve found that when teachers have the opportunity to talk about what they‚Äôve experienced, they actually can inspire each other to come up with solutions.‚Äù

    Malin emphasized that she sees educators as the experts when incorporating the purpose learning framework into their curriculum.
