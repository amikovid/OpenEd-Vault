## SEGMENT 1: THOUGHT (Isaac's voice)

**Source**: Joshua Fields Millburn podcast interview (dropping next week)

### Angle Options

**A. "Love Kids, Use Education"**
Isaac's reflection on Joshua's famous phrase "love people, use things" - flipping it to education. School, curricula, pedagogical approaches are tools. They're not the thing doing the driving. Your kid is.

> ISAAC: "Love people use things. And it made me think, man, that is actually really similar to what we talk about with parents at Open Ed all the time, which is like love kids use education."

**B. "The Apophatic Approach"**
Isaac's take on defining good parenting/education by what it's NOT. Rather than asking "what is good education?" - flip it: "what is NOT bad?" Don't do stuff you hate. Keep moving forward. You'll figure out what you love.

> ISAAC: "The apophatic approach to parenting... rather than asking what is good parenting or what is a good education, just flipping that around and saying, what is not bad?"

**C. "Hate Is Not the Same as Hard"**
Isaac's observation that kids working on Legos or beating a video game level shows they don't avoid hard things - they avoid meaningless things. The distinction matters for how we think about challenge in education.

> ISAAC: "I always tell young people with their career, don't do stuff you hate. But hate is not the same as hard."

**Recommended**: Angle A - "Love Kids, Use Education"

Why: It's the most quotable, directly ties to Joshua's famous framework, and makes the minimalism-education connection immediately clear. It's sticky because it repurposes something readers may already know.

**Structure**:
- Hook: Isaac's realization during conversation
- Substance: The quote and the insight
- Insight: Tools serve kids, not the reverse

## SEGMENT 2: TREND (Joshua's insights)

**Source**: Joshua Fields Millburn podcast interview

### Angle Options

**A. "No Shoulds, Only Coulds"**
Joshua's philosophy that there are no educational "shoulds" - only possibilities. Should my daughter learn karate? Take calculus? No shoulds. Only coulds. This reframes the entire conversation anxious parents have about what their kids "need."

> JOSHUA: "There are no shoulds, there are only coulds. So should my daughter be enrolled in a sport? Should my daughter learn karate? Should she dance? Should she learn an instrument? Should she take calculus at some point? No, there are no shoulds with respect to education."

**B. "Joy Cannot Be Acquired"**
Joshua's insight that peace and joy aren't things you add to life - they're buried beneath the clutter. Education parallel: we keep adding enrichment, activities, curricula - but maybe the breakthrough comes from removing what's in the way.

> JOSHUA: "You realize with minimalism the joy cannot be acquired. Peace cannot be acquired. It can only be uncovered. It's buried beneath the clutter."

**C. "Competition Is a Mental Illness"**
Joshua's provocative take that certain forms of competition represent a contract with yourself to be discontented until you get what you want - whether that's a prestigious kindergarten or the "right" college.

> JOSHUA: "I think that type of competition is a mental illness... It's like I'm signing a contract with myself to be discontented until I get the thing that I want."

**Recommended**: Angle A - "No Shoulds, Only Coulds"

Why: Most actionable for parents who are drowning in "should" anxiety. It's specific to education, contrarian without being hostile, and immediately applicable. Also directly complements the THOUGHT segment without repeating it.

**Structure**:
- Hook: The question every parent asks ("should my kid...")
- Substance: Joshua's reframe - shoulds vs. coulds
- Insight: Removing the pressure of imaginary requirements

## SEGMENT 3: TOOL

**Source**: Joshua Fields Millburn podcast interview

### Angle Options

**A. "The Entryway Rule"**
Practical: Joshua and his wife leave their phones at the door. They can still use them like a home phone, but distractions don't follow them around. Simple, concrete, immediately implementable.

> JOSHUA: "We have something called the entryway rule in our home... we leave our phone at the door. I can still use it like a home phone. I'm not deprived of the thing, but the distractions don't follow me around the house."

**B. "The Clutter Test" (Decision Framework)**
Apply to any educational decision: Is this curriculum/activity/schedule getting in the way of my child's joy and growth? If yes, it's clutter. Remove it. Joshua's coffee grinder example - it's not clutter until it's in your way.

> JOSHUA: "The way that I think about clutter is anything that gets in the way. Is our coffee grinder clutter? No, of course not. You use it every day. If I put it in your bed before you went to sleep, all of a sudden it would be clutter because it would get in your way."

**C. TEDx Talk: "Scrolling Is the New Smoking"**
The Minimalists quit social media for a year and had their most successful year ever. Link to the talk for pre-launch hype.

**Recommended**: Angle A - "The Entryway Rule"

Why: Most concrete and immediately actionable. Parents can implement this tonight. The Clutter Test is good but more abstract. TEDx talk is good for a P.S. mention but doesn't stand alone as a TOOL segment.

**Structure**:
- Hook: The problem (phones follow us everywhere)
- Substance: The rule and how it works
- Insight: You're not deprived - distractions just don't follow you

## BONUS: WORD OF THE DAY

**Pleonexia** [plee-oh-NEK-see-uh] (Greek)
Greed for things that can be counted.

> JOSHUA: "Ancient Greeks had a word for this, was pleonexia, which roughly translates to greed for things that can be counted."

**Education angle**: Grades, test scores, credits, accolades - all things that can be counted but don't measure joy or growth.
