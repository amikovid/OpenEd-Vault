## LinkedIn Posts

### LinkedIn #1: Contrarian Format

A 16-year-old filmmaker set out to document homeschooled teenagers.

He expected to find isolated kids being taught at kitchen tables.

Instead he found 10 teens crammed into a Manhattan apartment - reading Madame Bovary together. No parents. No teachers. No supervision.

Just kids who organized their own book club.

Mason Ember spent a year following homeschool teens in New York City for his documentary "Against the Grade." What surprised him most wasn't how different these kids were from each other.

It was what they had in common.

Every single one of them loved learning.

Not because someone forced them to. Because nobody had trained them to hate it yet.

The socialization question gets it backwards. These teenagers aren't lacking social skills - they're building communities that most adults couldn't organize.

Museum classes they coordinate themselves. Study groups where they set the curriculum. Friendships that started at a democratic school and turned into a jiu-jitsu crew.

His conclusion after interviewing dozens of homeschool families:

"Maybe it shouldn't be called homeschool. I'm out of the house for most of the day."

**CTA:** Have you seen unexpected community in alternative education? Drop it below.

### LinkedIn #2: Authority Format

Mason Ember is 16 years old.

He's also a preschool dropout.

At four, he was finger painting at an easel. His teacher said it was time for sing-along. He argued. He lost.

His parents pulled him out.

Twenty years from now, education researchers will still be debating what works. Homeschool vs. traditional. Structured vs. unschooled. Religious vs. secular.

Mason has a simpler framework.

"We should be pro-child. Not always pro-homeschooling. Certainly against one-size-fits-all institutionalism."

He wrote that in a letter to the New York Times after they published an article using one bad homeschool experience to condemn the entire practice.

His response: That's like finding a restaurant with health code violations and using it to define a city's entire food scene.

Any method can fail. Religious schools. Public schools. Homeschool. The failure isn't the method - it's ignoring what the kid actually needs.

Mason's advice lands with three words, repeated:

"Listen to the kid. Listen to the kid. Listen to the kid."

His documentary "Against the Grade" follows teens across the homeschool spectrum - from total academic structure to "I've never had to do anything I didn't want to."

Both approaches worked. Because both families listened.

**CTA:** What changed when you started listening instead of prescribing?
