## How to Start Homeschooling in Oregon

### Option 1: Traditional Homeschooling

If you want full control over every aspect of your child's education, traditional homeschooling lets you:

1. **Notify your local ESD** within 10 days of starting (find yours at [Oregon ESD Map](https://www.oaesd.org/))
2. **Choose your own curriculum** â€” or create your own
3. **Keep records** of instruction (recommended, not required)
4. **Complete state assessments** in grades 3, 5, 8, and 10

**Pros:** Maximum flexibility, complete curriculum control
**Cons:** You handle all the logistics yourself

### Option 2: Enroll in a Flexible Public Program

Several Oregon public programs support families who want to educate at home while accessing resources and support. These programs handle much of the administrative workâ€”you focus on your child's learning.

When enrolled in one of these programs, you're technically a student of the school, which means different (often simpler) requirements. You still educate at home with the flexibility to choose what works for your family.

## Programs Available in Oregon

Oregon offers several flexible public programs for families who want to learn at home. Here's an honest comparison:

### Virtual Charter Schools

Programs like **Oregon Connections Academy**, **Oregon Charter Academy**, and **Cascade Virtual Academy** provide:
- Full online curriculum (you use their materials)
- Certified teachers
- Structured coursework
- Flexible scheduling (no live attendance required)
- Free (public school)

**Best for:** Families who want a complete, ready-made curriculum with teacher support.

### Flexible Choice Programs

Programs like **OpenEd** and **Fossil Distance Learning Program** offer a different approach:
- You choose your own curriculum from approved options
- Access to educational resources at no cost
- Dedicated support (Educational Specialist or Mentor Teacher)
- Virtual classes, clubs, and enrichment opportunities
- Field trips and community events

**Best for:** Families who want to design a personalized education using resources that fit their child.

## Oregon Homeschool Laws: What You Need to Know

### Notification Requirements

You must notify your local Education Service District (ESD) within 10 days of:
- The start of the school year, OR
- Withdrawing your child from public/private school

The notification is simpleâ€”just a letter stating you intend to homeschool. Many ESDs have their own forms.

**Find your ESD:** [Oregon Association of ESDs](https://www.oaesd.org/)

### Required Subjects

Oregon does not mandate specific subjects for homeschoolers. You have complete freedom to design your curriculum.

### Assessment Requirements

Oregon requires standardized testing in grades 3, 5, 8, and 10. You can choose from several approved tests, and the results are for your recordsâ€”not reported to the state.

If your child scores below the 15th percentile, you may be asked to provide additional documentation, but this is rare.

### Recordkeeping

While not legally required, keeping records of your homeschool is strongly recommended:
- Attendance logs
- Work samples
- Curriculum used
- Test results

## Funding Your Education in Oregon

Oregon does not currently have a universal Education Savings Account (ESA) or voucher program for homeschoolers.

However, families enrolled in flexible public programs like OpenEd or Fossil DLP can access educational resources at no cost through their program's approved vendors and curriculum options.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need to be a certified teacher to homeschool in Oregon?

No. Oregon has no teacher qualification requirements for homeschool parents.

### Can my homeschooled child participate in public school sports?

Oregon law allows homeschooled students to participate in interscholastic activities at their local public school, subject to the same eligibility requirements as enrolled students. Contact your local district for specific policies.

### How do I get a homeschool diploma in Oregon?

Oregon homeschool parents can issue their own diploma. There is no state-issued homeschool diploma. For college applications, maintain transcripts and document coursework.

### Is homeschooling legal in Oregon?

Yes. Homeschooling has been legal in Oregon since 1985.

### What's the difference between homeschooling and enrolling in a flexible public program?

Traditional homeschoolers operate independentlyâ€”you notify the ESD, choose curriculum, and handle testing yourself.

Flexible public programs (like OpenEd or Fossil DLP) provide support, resources, and often handle administrative requirements. You're technically enrolled in a public school while educating at home. You're also paired with a certified teacher who can provide guidance, answer questions, and support your child's learningâ€”while you remain the primary educator.

Both paths let you learn at home with flexibility. The right choice depends on how much support you want.

### Does OpenEd offer high school in Oregon?

Currently, OpenEd Oregon serves grades K-8. An Oregon high school program is coming Fall 2026. For high school students right now, consider Fossil Distance Learning Program or one of the virtual charter schools listed above.
