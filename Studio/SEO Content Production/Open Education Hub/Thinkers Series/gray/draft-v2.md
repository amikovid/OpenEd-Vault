## ARTICLE

# Why Play Matters in Education: Peter Gray's Research on How Children Learn



For 99% of human history, children learned by playing. They climbed trees, built things, invented games with other kids, and figured out the world through trial and error. No worksheets. No grades. No adults hovering over their shoulders.

Then we introduced formal schooling, which inevitably hampered child development and learning. Peter Gray sought to find out what happened, and his research revealed a surprising fact: Children are biologically designed to educate themselves through play.

Gray, an American researcher and scholar, has spent decades studying what we lost in the classroom. His research draws on evolutionary biology, cross-cultural studies, and data from alternative schools to make a case that sounds radical but is actually ancient: The human species is built through play, and for kids, play equals learning  
"Play is, first and foremost, an expression of freedom," [Gray writes](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/freedom-learn/200811/the-value-play-i-the-definition-play-gives-insights). "It is what one wants to do as opposed to what one is obliged to do."

---

## How Play Evolved Into Stress

Gray's research on play draws from an unlikely source: hunter-gatherer societies. He surveyed anthropologists who had studied traditional cultures where children spend most of their time in free, mixed-age play.

What he found contradicted everything modern education assumes.

Hunter-gatherer children develop all the skills they need for adult life without formal instruction. They learn to hunt, gather, navigate complex social hierarchies, resolve conflicts, assess risk, and regulate their emotions—entirely through play. No one teaches them; they teach themselves.

"Play is quite literally the job of children," Gray explains. "It's how they're biologically programmed to learn."

The implications are uncomfortable for anyone invested in the current system. If children are natural learners who develop competence through self-directed play, what exactly is school adding to the equation?

According to Gray, often the answer is anxiety.

## The Hidden Curriculum of Stress

Gray tracks data that most education researchers ignore: the seasonal pattern of youth mental health.

Every year, mental health admissions for children and teenagers drop dramatically when school ends in late May. They spike again when school resumes in August. This pattern holds across decades of data, across different states, across economic backgrounds.

When researchers ask teenagers what stresses them out, 83% cite one thing: school. Not social media. Not peer pressure. Not family problems.

*School.*

"We as a culture would far rather blame social media than blame school," Gray observes. "School has a halo around it."

His critique isn't aimed at teachers or administrators. It's structural. The system itself—constant assessment, comparison to peers, mandatory attendance, credential-dispensing—creates anxiety by design.

"We have convinced children that how they're doing in school is a measure of them," Gray told us in [a recent podcast conversation](https://opened.co/podcast/every-august-youth-mental-health-crises-spike-every-june-they-plummet-its-not-a-coincidence). "They're constantly being judged. They're being made to feel like, 'If I am a failure at school, I'm a failure as a human being.'"

## How a Textbook Writer Became an Education Reformer

Gray didn’t just research the pitfalls of curriculum-based schooling. His family lived through it.

The researcher’s unorthodox path to educational advocacy started conventionally enough. He earned a Ph.D. in biological psychology and became a research professor at Boston College in 1972. For years, Gray was known primarily as the author of *Psychology*, a widely-used college textbook now in its eighth edition.

Then his son changed everything.

When Gray's son struggled in traditional school, the family enrolled him at [Sudbury Valley School](https://www.sudburyvalley.org/), a radically democratic school in Massachusetts where students direct their own education entirely. No curriculum, no required classes, and no grades. Students spend their days pursuing their interests while adults serve as resources, not directors.

Gray expected disaster but instead, he watched his son thrive.

"I began to see education in a whole different way," Gray has said. He started studying Sudbury Valley formally, surveying graduates to understand what happened when children were trusted to direct their own learning. The results surprised him: These students went on to college at typical rates, entered meaningful careers, and reported high life satisfaction.

What they didn't report was the anxiety and burnout that plague so many traditionally-schooled students.

## What Self-Directed Education Looks Like

Although Gray's critique of modern standardized schooling (aka the Prussian education model) is sharp, his proposed alternative is surprisingly gentle. He doesn't advocate for abandoning children to figure things out alone. He advocates for trusting them within supportive environments.

Self-directed education, as Gray defines it, happens when:

- Children pursue their own interests with adult support (not direction)  
- Learning emerges from intrinsic motivation rather than external pressure  
- Mixed-age interaction replaces same-age grouping  
- Assessment comes from the child's own sense of progress, not grades

This can happen in democratic schools like Sudbury Valley. It can happen through [unschooling](https://opened.co/blog/what-is-unschooling). It can happen in [microschools](https://opened.co/blog/a-beginners-guide-to-microschools-the-educational-trend-growing-faster-than-homeschooling) that prioritize autonomy over curriculum. Montessori-inspired schools and Reggio Emilia-based schools promote this type of child-centered learning, too.

Gray co-founded the [Alliance for Self-Directed Education](https://www.self-directed.org/) to connect families and educators pursuing these approaches. The organization offers resources, research, and community for those exploring alternatives to conventional schooling.

## How to Support Your Child’s Freedom to Learn

Gray’s 2013 book [*Free to Learn*](https://www.basicbooks.com/titles/peter-gray/free-to-learn/9780465084999/) became foundational reading for homeschooling families and alternative education advocates. But families whose children attend conventional schools can still put his advice into everyday practice:

### Stop being your child's calendar keeper.

Make them responsible for remembering their activities. "If you have to constantly remind them about an activity," Gray notes, "they're probably not genuinely interested in it."

### Let activities drop. 

If a child forgets an extracurricular commitment, don't rescue them. This reveals which activities they actually value versus which ones they feel pressured to attend.

### Encourage independent mobility.

Children should walk, bike, or take public transit when possible. "The idea that parents have to drive their kids everywhere—we're not doing anybody a favor."

### Give back unstructured time.

Many children are scheduled from morning to night. They need hours to simply be bored, explore, and discover what interests them without adult direction.

### Think relationship, not job.

Gray cites Alison Gopnik's observation that "parenting" as a verb didn't exist before 1970. "We shouldn't think of parenting as a job. It's a relationship, and children need respect and trust, not management."

## Debunking the Motivation Myth

One objection Gray hears constantly: "But my child isn't motivated."

His response is pointed: "Have you ever seen a truly non-self-motivated three-year-old or four-year-old? They're all self-motivated. They're always doing things. They're always curious."

The question, Gray suggests, is motivation for what.

A child who won't sit still for math worksheets might spend hours building elaborate LEGO structures, learning spatial reasoning, planning, and persistence along the way. A teenager who zones out during history lectures might devour books about World War II aircraft if allowed to follow their fascination.

"When we talk about the non-self-motivated child," Gray says, "what we often mean is they're not motivated to do the things we adults want them to do."

This distinction matters for parents [considering open education approaches](https://opened.co/blog/what-is-open-education). Structure still exists, but it emerges from the child's genuine interests rather than adult-imposed requirements.

## Self-Directed Learners Thrive in College

Parents often worry that self-directed education will close doors to higher education. Gray's research suggests the opposite.

His studies of Sudbury Valley graduates found that students who had never taken a traditional course, never received a grade, and never took an exam until possibly the SAT went on to attend and succeed in college. Some started at community colleges and transferred; others went directly to four-year institutions.

"Once they were there, it doesn't honestly surprise me that they can do well," Gray observes. "Every class starts pretty much from the beginning anyway. Introductory biology does not assume that you remember any biology from high school."

More importantly, students who have learned to take responsibility for their own education arrive at college with a crucial advantage: intrinsic motivation. They're there because they chose to be, not because their parents pushed them through. That makes all the difference.

## How to Follow Peter Gray’s Work

Now in his late 70s, Gray continues writing and researching. His [Psychology Today blog](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/freedom-learn) "Freedom to Learn" remains active, and his [Substack newsletter](https://petergray.substack.com/) "Play Makes Us Human" explores new research on play and education.

Gray’s next book, scheduled for September 2026, will trace the full arc of youth mental health from the 1950s to the present, including data most researchers ignore about periods when things actually improved.

The book expands on a point Gray made in his OpenEd interview: The current youth mental health crisis didn't start in 2010 with smartphones. It started in the 1950s when childhood began transforming into an adult-managed, scheduled, anxiety-producing experience.

"We made these changes gradually over time," Gray notes, "and people accepted it as normal because it was so gradual. If somebody had proposed those changes suddenly, there would have been a rebellion about it."
