There's a questioning impulse hiding under a lot of today's education coverage:

Can parents be trusted?

Every time a big outlet runs a story about a homeschooled student who "fell behind," the implication is the same: when families are left to themselves, kids get shortchanged.

These pieces follow a familiar pattern. Spotlight an anecdote. Quietly generalize it into a warning label. Skip the part that would actually help readers think clearly: what the broader data says.

Let's dive in.

SKIN IN THE GAME

Robert Enlow, president of EdChoice, has spent nearly 30 years watching what happens when families get more say in their kids' education.

His research shows something that applies whether you're homeschooling, using a private school, or within a public system: when parents have real input, outcomes improve. Students perform slightly better on state tests, graduate at higher rates, and attend college more often. But Enlow points to something that doesn't make headlines as often: the social effects.

"When you have skin in the game, you make better decisions," he says. Families who feel ownership get more involved. They engage more with teachers. Their kids show greater tolerance for differing opinions.

The schools that are thriving—public or private—are the ones that treat parents as partners, not obstacles. Give parents freedom and information, and they figure it out.

DATA VS. HEADLINES

Brett Pike runs @ClassicLearner and translates education research into clear, parent-friendly arguments. He's one of the best follows on the homeschool side of the internet.

After yet another recent mainstream story implied that homeschooling leads kids to fall behind, Pike responded with the simplest corrective possible:

"Homeschool students outperform public school peers in science, math, history, English, the ACT, and the SAT."

He's citing data from HSLDA's 2009 Progress Report, a nationwide study of nearly 12,000 homeschooled students conducted by Dr. Brian D. Ray. That study found homeschoolers scoring 34-39 percentile points above national averages. More than 78% of peer-reviewed studies since have shown the same pattern, and the advantage holds regardless of family income or parental education level.

This isn't obscure research. It's been replicated for decades. So why doesn't the coverage match the data?
