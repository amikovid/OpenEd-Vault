# Chapter 2: Students Aren't Standard

## Summary
This chapter dismantles the myth that standardized testing and one-size-fits-all education accurately measure learning. Using examples from elite athletes and research on human development, it reveals that standardized tests measure privilege and socioeconomic status, not capability. The chapter advocates for assessment systems that recognize individual differences and allow multiple pathways to demonstrate mastery.

The irony is that Einstein's genius emerged in spite of the education system, not because of it. The man who revolutionized physics was labeled a poor student and failed college entrance exams.

### Meeting the Critics Head On

Defenders of standardized testing argue we need objective measures for accountability. But this rests on misunderstandings about how humans learn, what learning matters in the modern world, and who's best positioned to determine if education is working.

Research shows employers want creativity, problem-solving, collaboration, and adaptability - none measurable through standardized tests. Companies like Google, Apple, and IBM have dropped degree requirements. Leading universities are going test-optional. Yet K-12 education still clings to testing that even AI can now answer.

### The Myth of Standardization as Rigor

Standardization is confused with rigor. True rigor looks different: feedback, practice, critique - the way athletes, musicians, and scientists actually develop mastery. At Sudbury Valley School, 90% of graduates go to higher education with most getting into first-choice colleges, despite never taking standardized tests.

### The False Promise of Same-Pace Learning

Brain development varies significantly. Children walk at different ages, speak at different times, learn to read at different paces. Yet our system forces identical pacing regardless of development. A 12-year-old classroom might have children ranging from 4'6" to 6'4" in height - all expected to learn at the same speed.

### The Student as Product

Ironically, most students advance regardless of mastery. The system doesn't care about learning - it cares about processing. Students are products flowing through a pipeline to college and then employment, with each transition demanding external validation metrics disconnected from real learning.

### The Transparency Problem

Standardized test vendors insist all questions remain secret under "testing fidelity." This creates absurdity: students prove knowledge without understanding what they need to know. Teachers must guess what's being tested. Parents are left in the dark.

Only the testing companies benefit - avoiding scrutiny while maintaining gatekeeping power.
